Television Energy Usage

Understanding the energy consumption of popular TV models in Australia.

How Much Energy Do Televisions Use?

Televisions vary widely in energy use depending on size, display technology, and power-saving features. LED TVs are generally more efficient than older LCD or plasma models.

Example Wattages

  • 32-inch LED TV — 40–55 W
  • 50-inch LED TV — 70–100 W
  • 65-inch LED TV — 120–160 W
  • Older Plasma TVs — 200–300 W+

Choosing an energy-efficient model can reduce long-term electricity costs, especially in homes where TVs are used several hours per day.

Data Insights: TV Energy Consumption

Distribution of TV Screen Sizes

The histogram shows that most televisions available in Australia fall between 120 cm and 150 cm. Smaller TVs are less common, while very large TVs appear less frequently in the dataset.

Screen Size vs Energy Consumption

The scatter plot shows a clear relationship between screen size and energy consumption. As screen size increases, energy usage also increases, meaning larger televisions generally consume more electricity.

Energy Consumption by Screen Technology

Different screen technologies such as LCD, LED, and OLED show variations in energy consumption. However, screen size appears to have a stronger impact on energy usage than technology alone.

Conclusion

This analysis shows that screen size has the greatest impact on television energy consumption. Larger TVs require significantly more power, which can increase electricity costs over time. Consumers should consider both screen size and energy efficiency ratings when purchasing a television.
